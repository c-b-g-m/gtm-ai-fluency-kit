# Facilitator Lite Guide

Use this guide to run a 30-minute internal AI Fluency 101 session. It works for teams of 4–15. No slides required.

---

## Before You Run This

Do the assessment yourself first. You'll be a better facilitator if you've been through it and can speak to what it surfaced for you. If you haven't done it yet, do `QUICKSTART-MANUAL.md` before prepping the session.

---

## Prep (Send 48 Hours Before)

Send attendees:
1. `FRAMEWORK-OVERVIEW.md` — 5-minute pre-read. Ask them to read it before the session.
2. Ask each person to come with **one recurring workflow they own** — something they do weekly or more often that involves writing, researching, reviewing, or communicating.
3. Ask each person to **choose their function track** — Marketing, Sales and RevOps, or Support and Customer Success.

Do not send the full rubric or assessment template as pre-work. That's too much. The pre-read + one workflow is enough.

---

## Session Agenda

### 0–5 min — Why AI Fluency Matters

**What to say:**
- "Most of us are using AI, but we're using it reactively — when we think of it, for whatever task is in front of us, with no real criteria for what good looks like."
- "The problem isn't access to tools. The problem is that we don't have a shared picture of what intentional AI use looks like in our roles. So we can't improve it, and we can't build on each other's work."
- "What we're doing today is establishing a baseline. Not a performance review — a starting point. You'll leave with a clearer picture of where you actually are and one concrete thing to work on."

**If someone pushes back ("we're already pretty good"):**
- "That may be true. The assessment will tell us. What it usually surfaces is that some dimensions are stronger than others — and that the gaps are specific, not general."

---

### 5–12 min — The 4D Model

**What to say:**
- "The framework measures four things — Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence. These aren't attitudes or effort scores. They're behaviors you can demonstrate."
- Walk through each dimension in one sentence, then give one team-relevant example:

  - **Delegation:** "Do you have clear criteria for what goes to AI and what stays human? Or do you use it when you think of it?"
  - **Description:** "Do you have reusable prompt patterns for your recurring work? Or do you write from scratch every time?"
  - **Discernment:** "Do you have explicit quality criteria before you review AI output? Or do you read it over and decide if it feels right?"
  - **Diligence:** "Do you have a consistent practice around what data you put into AI tools, and who's accountable for final output? Or is that handled case by case?"

- "Each dimension is scored 1 to 3. A 1 means reactive and one-off. A 2 means patterns are forming but inconsistent. A 3 means repeatable, documented, and demonstrable."
- "The critical rule: you can only score yourself a 3 if you can point to a real artifact — a prompt library, a decision log, a checklist you actually use. Not something you'd do if asked. Something that already exists."

**If the framework overview was read as pre-work:** Ask "Did anything from the pre-read resonate or surprise you?" Take one or two responses. This warms the room and signals it's a real conversation, not a lecture.

---

### 12–20 min — Role-Specific Examples

**What to say:**
- "Let's make this concrete. Open `assessment/calibrated-scoring-examples.md` and find your function track. Read the examples for one dimension — pick whichever one you're most curious about."
- Give them 3 minutes to read.
- Ask: "Who found an example that matched where they are? What level?" Take 3–4 responses.
- Ask: "Did any example surprise you — either because it was lower than you expected or higher?" This usually generates good discussion about where people's mental models diverge.

**What to listen for:**
- People who identify with level 2 examples but assumed they were at level 3 — that's the most common and most valuable insight.
- People who are genuinely at level 1 in a dimension they thought was fine — handle this with care; it can feel exposing in a group setting.

**If pressed on "what does level 3 actually require":**
- Redirect to the evidence rule: "The question isn't whether you do this — it's whether you can show it. A prompt library you can open right now. A decision log from last week. That's the bar."

---

### 20–26 min — Assessment Walkthrough

**What to say:**
- "We're not going to complete the full assessment right now — that's individual work that takes 20–30 minutes. What we're going to do is score one dimension together so you understand the process."
- "Pick Delegation. Open `assessment/rubric.md`. Read the criteria for 1, 2, and 3. Be honest with yourself."
- Give them 2 minutes.
- Ask: "Without sharing specific numbers yet — how many people feel confident in their score? How many feel uncertain?" (Show of hands or verbal.)
- "Uncertainty is normal. It usually means you're between levels or you don't have strong evidence yet. Both are useful signals."
- "After today, each of you will complete the full assessment on your own using `QUICKSTART-MANUAL.md`. It covers all four dimensions and walks you through building your 30/60/90 plan."

**If time allows:** Have attendees share their Delegation score with a partner and gut-check each other. "Does that match what you observe about how they work?" Peer calibration in 60 seconds per person is more valuable than any facilitator explanation.

---

### 26–30 min — 30-Day Commitment

**What to say:**
- "Before we close, I want each person to name one thing. Not a goal — a workflow. One recurring thing you own that you're going to apply this framework to."
- Give 90 seconds for people to write it down.
- Go around the room (or use chat in remote sessions): each person states their workflow in one sentence.
- Apply the non-generic check out loud for each one: "Is that measurable? Can you tell in 30 days whether it improved?"
  - If someone says "use AI more in my sales prep" — push: "More specifically — which part of sales prep? What would you measure?"
  - If someone says "reduce time on weekly pipeline reports using AI" — that's a pass.
- Close: "Complete your full assessment this week. Come to our 2-week check-in with your scores and your first-draft 30/60/90 plan."

---

## After the Session

**Collect commitments:** Ask each attendee to send you their workflow statement in writing before end of day. This creates accountability without requiring a formal tracking system.

**2-week check-in (15 minutes):**
- "Did you complete the assessment? What were your two lowest scores?"
- "What's one thing you've done in the last two weeks toward your 30-day plan?"
- "What's blocking you?" (Common answers: "I haven't found time," "I wasn't sure how to start," "My plan felt too generic." All addressable.)
- If most people haven't done the assessment: re-send `QUICKSTART-MANUAL.md` with a specific ask: "Complete Steps 1–5 before Friday and bring your completed learning plan to the 30-day check-in."

**30-day check-in (20 minutes):**
- "What's one artifact you can show — a prompt you saved, a decision log, a workflow you documented?"
- "Did your 30-day goal produce the result you expected? Why or why not?"
- "What's your 60-day focus based on what you learned?" Have each person state a specific next milestone, not a vague intention.
- This is the most important check-in. If someone arrives with no artifact, that's the conversation — not a failure, but a signal that the plan was too abstract. Help them make it concrete before day 31.

**60- and 90-day follow-up:**
- These don't need a dedicated session. A Slack thread or async check-in works: "Show us one thing you built or changed in the last 30 days."
- By day 60, people should be moving from documenting workflows to running them consistently. By day 90, the signal is whether AI use has changed any measurable output in their role (cycle time, error rate, volume, quality score).
- If your team is using 1:1s or sprint reviews, these milestones can fold into existing rhythms — the framework doesn't require its own dedicated meetings after day 30.

**What not to do:**
- Don't collect everyone's scores and make a team-level dashboard. That shifts the frame from personal development to performance measurement, which kills honesty.
- Don't skip the 2-week check-in. The 30-day plan only works if someone asks about it at 14 days.
- Don't let the process end at 30 days. The 30/60/90 structure only delivers value if all three phases close.

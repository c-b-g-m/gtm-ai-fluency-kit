# How to Score Your Assessment

The 4D rubric uses a 1–4 scale per dimension. This guide walks through common questions and failure modes so your score reflects reality, not aspiration.

---

## Who Should Score

**Start with self-assessment.** You know your workflows better than anyone. Use the rubric and calibrated examples to anchor yourself honestly.

**Validate with a peer if you can.** Share your filled-in assessment with a colleague who sees your work regularly. Ask them: "Does this match what you observe?" Peer review catches blind spots — especially in Discernment and Diligence, where people tend to overestimate.

**Manager review is optional for individual use.** If you're running this as a team baseline, manager review helps calibrate across the team and surfaces coaching opportunities.

---

## The Evidence Rule

**If you can't point to a concrete artifact, don't score 3 or 4.**

A score of 3 (Capable) means you have a repeatable, documented workflow you can demonstrate. A score of 4 (Adoptive) means you've built something durable and shared it with others.

Artifacts that count:
- A prompt library or saved prompt templates
- A before/after output sample with your edits marked
- A decision log (what you kept, changed, or discarded and why)
- A diligence statement or review checklist in active use
- A workflow map, automation, or process doc you reference regularly

Artifacts that don't count:
- Describing what you *would* do
- A one-time example you remember but can't reproduce
- General awareness of best practices

---

## When You're Between Levels

Pick the lower score unless you have evidence for the higher one.

The rubric describes each level as a floor, not a ceiling. Being "mostly a 2 with some 3 behaviors" means you're a 2. You move up when the higher-level behavior is consistent and documented, not occasional.

---

## Dimension-Specific Watch-Outs

**Delegation**
Common overestimate: "I use AI for lots of things" scores as a 3. Using AI frequently isn't the same as using it intentionally. A 3 requires explicit decision criteria for what goes to AI and what stays human — not just habit.

**Description**
Common overestimate: "I revise my prompts when they don't work" scores as a 2, not a 3. A 3 requires reusable, documented prompt structures that you apply consistently. Iteration counts; a library is what earns the higher score.

**Discernment**
Common overestimate: "I read it before I use it" scores as a 1 or 2. A 3 requires explicit quality criteria defined in advance — not just a gut check after the fact. If you can't describe your criteria before you see the output, you're below a 3.

**Diligence**
Common underestimate: people score themselves low here because disclosure and governance feel like "work stuff, not AI stuff." If you consistently name review ownership, run a basic privacy check before pasting customer data into a prompt, and flag AI involvement in your handoffs — that's a 3.

---

## After You Score

Your lowest two dimensions are your 30-day focus. You don't need to improve everything at once. Move one level on one dimension before expanding.

Use `assessment/rubric.md` to read what the next level up requires, then write actions in your learning plan that would produce that evidence. If you can't write a concrete action that would generate a real artifact, the action is too vague.

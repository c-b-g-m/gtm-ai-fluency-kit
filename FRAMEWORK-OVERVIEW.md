# AI Fluency Framework Overview

Share this as a pre-read before a team session, or read it before starting your assessment. Takes about 5 minutes.

---

## What AI Fluency Actually Means

AI fluency isn't about knowing which tools exist or being able to write a prompt. It's about using AI intentionally — knowing when to use it, how to direct it well, how to catch what it gets wrong, and how to stay accountable for what you ship.

Most GTM professionals are somewhere on the spectrum between "I use it sometimes" and "I have a system." This framework helps you see exactly where you are and what moves you forward.

---

## The 4D Model

The framework measures fluency across four dimensions. Each one describes a behavior you can observe and demonstrate — not a mindset or a vague competency.

---

### Delegation
*Knowing what to give to AI, what to keep human, and why.*

This isn't about using AI more — it's about using it with intention. A low score here looks like: "I use AI when I think of it." A high score looks like: "I have explicit criteria for which tasks I delegate, what context I provide, and what I expect back."

**In practice:**
A Marketing Manager with weak Delegation asks AI for a campaign headline whenever she's stuck. A Marketing Manager with strong Delegation has a documented workflow: here's the brief format I give AI, here are the constraints, here's what I do if the output misses.

---

### Description
*How well you communicate with AI to get useful output.*

Quality prompting isn't just about better phrasing — it's about providing the right context, iterating deliberately, and building reusable patterns so you're not starting from scratch every time.

**In practice:**
A weak Description score looks like: writes a new prompt from memory each time, revises when something is wrong but can't articulate what changed. A strong score looks like: maintains a prompt library, can show before/after examples, knows which variables in a prompt change the output most.

---

### Discernment
*Catching what AI gets wrong before it causes a problem.*

AI output is often mostly right and partially wrong. Discernment is the habit of evaluating output against explicit criteria — not just reading it over and deciding it "feels okay."

**In practice:**
A weak Discernment score looks like: "I read it before I send it." A strong score looks like: "I have a defined review pass — I check for factual accuracy, then audience fit, then compliance risk — and I log what I changed and why."

---

### Diligence
*Staying accountable for what AI produces on your behalf.*

This covers disclosure, data privacy, and ownership. Who reviews before something ships? What data are you putting into AI tools? When do you flag AI involvement to colleagues or clients?

**In practice:**
A weak Diligence score looks like: minimal thought about what data goes into prompts, no consistent disclosure practice, no named reviewer. A strong score looks like: runs a data check before every prompt, flags AI involvement in internal handoffs, is named as the accountable owner for review before anything ships.

---

## Three Maturity Levels

Each dimension is scored 1–3:

| Level | What It Means |
|-------|---------------|
| **1 — Emerging** | Reactive, one-off use. No repeatable process. Limited quality checks. |
| **2 — Developing** | Some patterns forming. Inconsistent criteria. Diligence applied in higher-risk situations but not by default. |
| **3 — Capable** | Repeatable workflows. Explicit quality criteria. Accountable habits applied consistently. Evidence available. |

**The key rule:** Don't score yourself at 3 unless you can point to a concrete artifact — a prompt library, a decision log, a checklist in active use. A 3 means you can demonstrate it, not just describe it.

---

## What This Is Not

- It's not a tool evaluation. What tools you use doesn't affect your score.
- It's not a test of effort. Someone who uses AI rarely but intentionally scores higher than someone who uses it constantly without criteria.
- It's not an attitude check. The framework measures observable behaviors, not enthusiasm.
